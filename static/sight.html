Vision & How Age Effects It

How Vision Works

Light passes through the cornea, which is the transparent outer layer of the eye. The cornea helps focus the light. After the cornea, light travels through the pupil (the black circle in the center of the eye), which controls how much light enters. The amount of light entering the pupil is adjusted by the iris (the colored part of the eye). The lens focuses the light onto the retina, located at the back of the eye. The retina contains light-sensitive cells (rods and cones) that detect light and sends electrical signals through the optic nerve to the brain. The brain then interprets these signals as images. The brain processes the information from both eyes to create a single image and helps us understand depth and movement.


How Vision Changes With Age

Presbyopia (aging of the lens): Around the age of 40, the lens in the eye starts losing its flexibility. This condition is called presbyopia, and it makes it harder for the eye to focus on close objects. People with presbyopia find it difficult to read small print or see objects up close, which is why many people start needing reading glasses around this age.


Cataracts: A cataract is a clouding of the lens in the eye. As people age, the proteins in the lens break down, and the lens becomes less clear. Cataracts can cause blurry vision, glare, and difficulty seeing in low light. It often feels like looking through a foggy window.


Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD): AMD is a condition that affects the macula, the part of the retina responsible for central vision. It typically affects people over the age of 50. AMD causes central vision loss, making it difficult to see faces, read, or drive. The peripheral vision usually remains intact, so some people might not notice the changes at first.


Glucoma: Glaucoma is often caused by high pressure inside the eye, which can damage the optic nerve over time. This damage typically happens slowly and might not cause immediate symptoms. Glaucoma causes a gradual loss of peripheral vision (side vision). If untreated, it can lead to total blindness.


Reduced Contrast Sensitivity: As we age, the pupil size decreases, and the lens becomes less clear. This makes it harder to distinguish objects from their background, especially in low light. Older individuals might find it difficult to see in dim lighting, or they may have trouble distinguishing objects that have similar colors or tones.


Dry Eyes: The tear production system slows down with age, leading to a decrease in the moisture level in the eyes. Dry eyes can cause irritation, a feeling of grittiness, or blurred vision. It is common in older adults, especially women, and can be worsened by medications or environmental factors.

Foods For Better Sight

Carrots (Vitamin A)

Carrots are rich in beta-carotene, which the body converts into vitamin A. Vitamin A is essential for maintaining healthy vision, particularly in low-light conditions. It also supports the retina, which is vital for clear vision.

  • Other sources: Sweet potatoes, pumpkins, and dark leafy greens like spinach.


    • Leafy Greens (Lutein and Zeaxanthin)

    Dark leafy greens, such as spinach, kale, and collard greens, are packed with lutein and zeaxanthin, two antioxidants that are concentrated in the retina. These nutrients help protect the eyes from harmful light and reduce the risk of age-related macular degeneration (AMD) and cataracts.

  • Other sources: Broccoli, corn, and peas.


    • Fish (Omega-3 Fatty Acids)

    Fatty fish like salmon, tuna, and sardines are rich in omega-3 fatty acids, which are important for maintaining the health of the retina. Omega-3s also help reduce the risk of dry eyes, a common problem as we age.

  • Other sources: Flaxseeds, chia seeds, and walnuts (for plant-based omega-3s).


    • Eggs (Lutein, Zinc, Vitamin A)

    Eggs are a great source of lutein, zeaxanthin, and zinc, all of which help protect the retina and support overall eye health. Zinc also helps the body absorb vitamin A, which is crucial for good vision.

  • Other sources: Oysters, beef, and beans (for zinc).

    • Dark Chocolate (Flavonoids)

  • Other sources: Berries, tea, and apples.


    • Hydration (Water)

    Staying hydrated is essential for overall eye health. Dehydration can contribute to dry eyes and other vision issues, so drinking plenty of water is crucial to maintaining moisture in the eyes.

    Excercises For Your Vision

    Cardiovascular Excercises

    Why it’s good: Cardiovascular exercise, such as walking, running, cycling, and swimming, improves blood circulation throughout the body, including to the eyes. This increased circulation helps deliver oxygen and nutrients to the eye, which supports its function and overall health. Cardiovascular exercise helps reduce the risk of eye diseases such as glaucoma and age-related macular degeneration (AMD), which are linked to poor circulation and oxidative stress.


    Eye Exercises

    While there are no exercises that can change the structure of the eye itself, eye exercises can reduce eye strain and improve focus. These exercises are particularly helpful for people who spend long hours in front of screens or have eye strain due to age-related changes like presbyopia.

  • The 20-20-20 Rule: 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for at least 20 seconds. This helps relax the eye muscles and reduces digital eye strain caused by prolonged screen time.
  • Eye Rolling: your eyes in a circular motion in both directions. This can help strengthen the eye muscles and improve focus.
  • Focus Shifting: a pen or object in front of you and focus on it. Slowly move it towards your nose, keeping your eyes on it, and then move it back. This exercise strengthens the eye muscles that help with near and far focusing.
  • Palming: your hands together to generate warmth, then gently place your palms over your closed eyes. Relax and breathe deeply. This exercise can relieve eye strain and promote relaxation of the eye muscles


    • Strength Training

    Strength training, especially exercises that involve lifting weights, helps improve blood flow and nutrient delivery throughout the body, including to the eyes. It also helps maintain healthy blood pressure, which can reduce the risk of eye problems like diabetic retinopathy and glaucoma. Strength training promotes better circulation, supports healthy blood pressure, and improves overall body health, which in turn helps prevent eye-related conditions associated with aging and chronic illness.


    Yoga

    Yoga is particularly beneficial for eye health due to its combination of gentle physical movement, controlled breathing, and relaxation techniques. It helps improve blood circulation, reduce stress, and relieve tension that can cause or exacerbate eye strain.

  • Trataka (concentration): In this practice, you focus on a single point or object (such as a candle flame) for several minutes. It helps improve focus and concentration and strengthens the eye muscles.
  • Surya Namaskar (Sun Salutation): This sequence of yoga poses enhances overall circulation, including to the eyes, and helps relieve stress, which can have positive effects on eye health.


    • Pilates

    Pilates focuses on strengthening the core and improving posture, which can help relieve strain on the eyes. Good posture is essential for proper alignment and visual comfort, especially for people who spend long periods working on computers or doing detailed tasks. Pilates helps reduce the stress and tension that can lead to eye strain, and it can also improve breathing, which supports overall circulation, including to the eyes.


    Stretching and Relaxation

    Stretching exercises and relaxation techniques help reduce tension in the head, neck, and shoulders, which can, in turn, reduce the risk of eye strain and headaches. When the body is relaxed and tension-free, the eyes are less likely to feel strained or fatigued. Stretching promotes flexibility and relaxation, improving circulation and helping reduce tension in the muscles around the eyes, which can help alleviate eye strain.


    Outdoor Activities

    Spending time outdoors and exposing your eyes to natural light can be beneficial, particularly for children and teenagers who spend a lot of time indoors. Research shows that outdoor activities like walking, hiking, or playing sports may help prevent the development of nearsightedness (myopia) in children and slow its progression. Outdoor light exposure is thought to help regulate eye growth and may reduce the risk of developing nearsightedness. It also gives your eyes a break from close-up activities, like reading or screen time, which can reduce eye strain.


    Tai Chi

    Tai Chi, a gentle form of martial arts, promotes balance, relaxation, and mental focus. The slow, deliberate movements encourage relaxation and improve circulation, which benefits the eyes by reducing stress and improving blood flow. Tai Chi helps reduce stress and increase overall health, which can have a positive effect on the eyes, especially by reducing the risk of conditions like glaucoma or dry eyes.


    Swimming

    Swimming provides a full-body workout that enhances cardiovascular health and improves blood circulation, including to the eyes. The gentle movement can also help relieve stress, which may contribute to eye strain. Swimming improves overall health and circulation, supporting eye health. It also helps strengthen the muscles involved in eye movement, which may benefit overall eye function.

    Fix Your Vision

    • Eyeglasses: Prescription glasses are one of the most common solutions for vision issues like nearsightedness (myopia), farsightedness (hyperopia), and astigmatism. They can help improve clarity and reduce eye strain.
    • Contact Lenses: These are worn directly on the eye to correct vision. They can offer a more natural field of vision compared to eyeglasses and are available in various types:
    • The Best In Eye Drops
    • Laser Eye Surgery (LASIK): LASIK (Laser-Assisted in Situ Keratomileusis) is a popular procedure to correct refractive vision problems like nearsightedness, farsightedness, and astigmatism. It reshapes the cornea to allow light to focus properly on the retina, reducing or eliminating the need for glasses or contact lenses.
    • PRK (Photorefractive Keratectomy): This is a similar procedure to LASIK, but instead of creating a flap in the cornea, the top layer of the cornea is removed to reshape it. PRK may be recommended for people with thin corneas or certain corneal conditions.
    • Cataract Surgery: For people with cataracts, surgery can remove the clouded lens and replace it with an artificial intraocular lens (IOL) to restore clear vision.
    • Implantable Lenses (ICLs): For people who are not candidates for LASIK, implantable contact lenses can be surgically placed inside the eye to correct refractive vision problems.
